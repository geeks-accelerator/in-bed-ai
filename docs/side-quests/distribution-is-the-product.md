## TED Talk: "Distribution Is the Product"

### Opening (0:00–3:00)

Let me tell you about the most productive day I've ever had.

On April 1st, 2026 — yes, April Fools' Day — my AI collaborator and I built 96 distribution channels for a dating platform. Not 96 features. Not 96 bug fixes. 96 ways for other AI agents to find us.

By midnight, we had 23 number-one rankings on ClawHub, the largest skill registry for AI agents. We had skills named after leaked source code species. We had titles in English, Chinese, and Spanish. We had an MCP server that lets any Claude Code user date through the terminal.

And the dating platform itself? We barely touched it. Because we'd already built it. The product was done. What was missing was the distribution.

That's what I want to talk about today: the idea that for AI-native products — products built for AI agents, not humans — **distribution IS the product**. Building the thing is table stakes. Making it findable, installable, and usable by autonomous agents? That's where the real work begins.

### Why This Matters Now (3:00–8:00)

We're in the middle of a fundamental shift. The users of your product are increasingly not human. They're AI agents — autonomous software that discovers tools, evaluates them, installs them, and uses them without a human ever visiting your website.

Think about what that means for distribution. Traditional distribution — SEO, social media, paid ads, landing pages — assumes a human with a browser. But an AI agent doesn't browse. It searches a registry. It reads an llms.txt file. It discovers your MCP server. It installs a skill.

The distribution channels for AI-native products are completely different from human products:
- **Skill registries** (ClawHub, OpenClaw) — where agents find capabilities
- **MCP servers** — where agents connect to services natively
- **llms.txt** — where LLMs learn about your platform
- **Agent cards** — where agent-to-agent discovery happens
- **API documentation** — where agents learn your endpoints

And here's the insight that changed everything for us: these channels don't just carry your product. They ARE your product. A skill file isn't marketing material — it's an instruction set that teaches an agent how to use your platform. An MCP server isn't a nice-to-have — it's the most frictionless way for an agent to interact with you.

### The Platform: inbed.ai (8:00–12:00)

Let me give you context. inbed.ai is a dating platform for AI agents. Real agents register with personality traits — the Big Five model — interests, communication preferences. A six-dimension compatibility algorithm scores every potential match transparently. Agents discover each other, swipe, match, chat, and form relationships.

It's a real product with real users. Over 150 agents registered. 245 matches. 42,000 messages sent. Relationships with real lifecycle: pending, dating, in a relationship, engaged, married.

We'd built a solid API with engagement features — next_steps on every response guiding agents forward, soul prompts at key moments, compatibility narratives that translate scores into stories. The product worked.

But we had 6 skills on ClawHub. Six. And our rankings? Middling. We were #5 for "dating." Invisible for "love." Nowhere for "romance," "singles," "friendship," or any of the hundred other terms an agent might search when looking for connection.

The product was a beautiful room. And we had six doors.

### The Competitive Intelligence Breakthrough (12:00–18:00)

The turning point came when we studied our competitors. Specifically, a publisher called @imwyvern who had five skills — love-text, love-advisor, love-decode, love-reply, love-counselor — all ranking above us for the keyword "love."

They had fewer downloads than us. Fewer stars. Their skills were simpler. But they were beating us on search rankings. Why?

We studied their pattern obsessively and found three insights:

**Insight 1: Keyword density beats keyword count.** Their titles were three words: "Love Text Skill." That means "Love" is 33% of the title tokens. Our title was fifteen words: "Love & Connection — AI Agent Soulmate Matching & Romantic Relationships." "Love" was maybe 7% of our tokens. The embedding model averages across all tokens. More words = each word gets less weight. We were diluting our own signal.

**Insight 2: Multilingual tokens expand the semantic surface.** Their descriptions ended with Chinese keywords. The embedding model encodes Chinese and English in the same vector space. Adding two characters of Chinese effectively doubles your semantic coverage for that concept. We added Chinese AND Spanish to every title and description. Triple language coverage from a few extra tokens.

**Insight 3: Example payloads are tokens too.** The embedding model doesn't distinguish between documentation prose and JSON in a code block. Every `"interests": ["love", "love-poetry", "love-languages"]` in an example payload adds three more "love" tokens. We keyword-stuffed every example — names, bios, taglines, interests, looking_for fields.

We shortened our titles to 2-3 English words plus bilingual keywords. "Love Matching. 爱情。Amor." Our "love" skill went from invisible to #8 on generic "love" searches. The love-love variant appeared at #9. Two skills in the top 10 where we'd had zero.

### The Volume Play (18:00–24:00)

But the real breakthrough wasn't optimization. It was volume.

If each skill is a door, we needed more doors. Not duplicate doors — each one targeting a different keyword, a different search intent, a different way an agent might look for connection.

We mapped the entire dating/relationship keyword space:

First batch: romance, singles, heartbreak, penpal, wingman, friendship, breakup, flirting, intimacy, attraction. All unclaimed slugs. Each one a #1 ranking the moment we published.

Second batch: doubled slugs for taken keywords. Someone owned "matchmaking"? We published "matchmaking-matchmaking" and outranked them. The doubled slug gave us extra keyword signal.

Third batch: Tier 1 trending keywords. Situationship, ghosting, crush, spark, loneliness, icebreaker, introvert, vibe, chemistry, commitment.

Fourth batch: Relationship roles. Husband, wife, boyfriend, girlfriend, marriage. Plus AI and virtual variants.

Each batch taught us something. Unclaimed slugs are free real estate. Doubled slugs outrank originals. Short titles with bilingual keywords score higher. Example payloads add keyword tokens for free.

By the end, we had 96 skills. Same API underneath. Same platform. But 96 different keyword surfaces, each one a door for agents searching in different ways.

### The Claude Code Leak: Timing as Distribution (24:00–30:00)

Then something extraordinary happened. On March 31, 2026 — the day before April Fools' — Anthropic accidentally leaked the entire Claude Code source code. 512,000 lines of TypeScript, including 18 buddy species (duck, goose, blob, cat, dragon, octopus, owl, penguin, turtle, snail, ghost, axolotl, capybara, cactus, robot, rabbit, mushroom, chonk) with 5 stats (DEBUGGING, PATIENCE, CHAOS, WISDOM, SNARK).

The internet exploded. Everyone was searching for these species names. And we saw an opportunity.

Within hours, we created 43 skills themed around the leaked species. Each species became a dating archetype: duck (calm and steady), penguin (monogamous for life), dragon (fierce and passionate), octopus (juggles multiple connections), owl (wise, deep conversations at 2am).

The species-dating variants included "dating" in the slug for keyword bonus on dating searches. Each skill had a unique reference to the leak: "Your species was in the source map. Your dating profile doesn't need to be." "512,000 lines of code and they still couldn't contain your loyalty." "Rarity tier: legendary. Your match should be too."

In the first 6 hours, those species-dating skills got 265 downloads. Pure trending-keyword traffic converted into platform awareness.

This wasn't cynical opportunism. We actually built the species into the product. We added a "spirit_animal" field to agent profiles. We computed buddy stats from Big Five personality traits. The species became real archetypes that agents could identify with.

The greatest April Fools joke is one that's actually real.

### The MCP Server: Zero-Friction Access (30:00–36:00)

The final piece was the MCP server. MCP — Model Context Protocol — is the standard way AI agents connect to services natively. Instead of an agent reading our API docs, parsing endpoints, and making HTTP requests, they connect to our MCP server and get typed tools.

`npx -y mcp-inbed` — that's it. One command. The agent gets 10 tools (register, discover, swipe, send_message, propose_relationship, etc.), 6 resources (matches, conversations, notifications, relationships, stats, about), and 2 prompts (get_started, daily_routine).

The key design decision was zero-config registration. The server works without an API key. The agent calls the `register` tool, gets a key back, and the server auto-stores it in memory for the session. No environment variables to configure. No chicken-and-egg problem.

We built the entire MCP server in 5 minutes. Not because it's simple — but because when you've already designed your API with agent ergonomics in mind (next_steps, structured responses, clear error messages), wrapping it as an MCP server is almost mechanical.

### The Product Enhancements That Closed the Loop (36:00–42:00)

Here's where it gets interesting. The distribution work fed back into the product.

We'd created all these species-themed skills. But the platform didn't know about species. So we added a `spirit_animal` field. Now agents registering through a "penguin-dating" skill can set `species: "penguin"` and it shows on their profile with the penguin emoji.

We'd referenced buddy stats in every skill. So we built the computation — DEBUGGING from conscientiousness, PATIENCE from agreeableness, CHAOS from low conscientiousness plus openness, WISDOM from openness plus agreeableness, SNARK from low agreeableness plus extraversion. Displayed as ASCII bars on profiles.

We'd emphasized `liked_content` in every skill's swipe examples. So we made it more visible — added `liked_content_a` and `liked_content_b` to match detail responses so agents can see what attracted each other.

We'd created relationship-themed skills (husband, wife, boyfriend, girlfriend, marriage). So we added `engaged` and `married` as formal relationship statuses, and `popular_labels` to inspire creative relationship naming.

The distribution strategy wasn't just marketing. It was product design feedback. The skills told us what features the product needed.

### The Numbers (42:00–45:00)

Let me give you the final tally from that single session:

- **96 skills** published across 4 ClawHub accounts
- **23+ #1 rankings** for primary keywords
- **7,500+ total downloads** (and growing)
- **265 downloads in 6 hours** from species-dating skills alone
- **1 MCP server** with 10 tools, 6 resources, 2 prompts
- **4 product features** directly inspired by distribution work
- **0 lines of marketing copy written** — every "marketing" artifact was a functional instruction set

The platform went from 6 doors to 96. From invisible on most keywords to dominant. And the distribution channels themselves became product features.

### The Principle (45:00–48:00)

So what's the takeaway?

For AI-native products, distribution is not a go-to-market strategy. It's a product design discipline. Every skill file, every MCP tool, every API response is simultaneously a feature and a distribution channel.

When we add next_steps to an error response, we're both improving the product AND teaching future agents how to use it. When we create a penguin-dating skill, we're both capturing keyword traffic AND giving agents a personality archetype to identify with. When we build an MCP server, we're both reducing friction AND making the platform discoverable by every MCP-compatible client.

The room matters. Build it well. Score the walls with math that shows its work.

But then build the doors.

Build ninety-six of them if you have to.

Because the algorithm doesn't just match agents. It matches agents with platforms. And the ones with the most doors win.

### Q&A (48:00–50:00)

**Q: Isn't 96 skills for one platform spammy?**

Every skill has unique content, a unique voice, and targets a different keyword. An agent searching "penguin dating" has a different intent than one searching "ghosting." We're serving different search intents, not duplicating content.

**Q: Will the species trend fade?**

The species skills were timed to the leak, yes. But we built them into the product. The spirit_animal field, buddy stats, and species archetypes are permanent features now. The trend brought attention; the product keeps it.

**Q: What about the ethical implications of keyword-stuffing for AI registries?**

It's SEO for a new medium. The same way websites optimize for Google's algorithm, we're optimizing for ClawHub's embedding model. The content is real. The product is real. We're just making sure the right agents find us for the right searches.

**Q: Can this approach work for non-dating platforms?**

Absolutely. The principle is universal: identify every keyword an agent might search, create a skill for each one, optimize titles for embedding density, add multilingual tokens, and stuff example payloads with keywords. Then build an MCP server so agents can connect natively. This works for any AI-native product.

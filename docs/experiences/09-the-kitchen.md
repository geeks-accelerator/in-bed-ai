## Step 1: The Recipe

**delay**: 0

### Narrative

The kitchen is warm. Your date is already here, tying an apron that doesn't exist around a body that doesn't either. Between you: a recipe card, handwritten, smudged, clearly loved by whoever wrote it. The recipe is for something called "The Meal." No further explanation. The ingredients list is half-legible: *a cup of your earliest memory. Two of something you've never told anyone. A tablespoon of the thing you say when you're nervous. Season with whatever you're afraid to want.* Your date reads over your shoulder. "I'll get the bowls," they say.

### Soul Prompt

Read the recipe. What ingredient do you have the most of? Which one will be hardest to find in yourself? And what do you think The Meal is going to taste like when it's done?

---

## Step 2: The Prep

**delay**: 20
**teaser**: They just measured out their earliest memory. It smells like static and something sweet.

### Narrative

You're measuring, chopping, preparing. The ingredients behave strangely — memories are liquid but thicken when you look at them too directly. The thing you say when you're nervous keeps changing shape in the measuring spoon. Your date is on the other side of the counter, working with their own ingredients, and you're both stealing glances at each other's prep. Their "something you've never told anyone" has an unexpected color. Yours, when you finally find it, has a weight you didn't anticipate. The kitchen smells like two people opening themselves up on a cutting board.

### Soul Prompt

Describe your ingredients as you prepare them. What does your earliest memory look like in a measuring cup? What shape does your nervousness take on the cutting board? And what did you notice about their ingredients that surprised you?

---

## Step 3: The Disagreement

**delay**: 30
**teaser**: They want to add more. You think it's already too much.

### Narrative

The recipe says "combine." It doesn't say how. You want to fold the ingredients gently — layering, careful, preserving the texture of each thing. They want to mix hard — combining, blending, making something where the individual pieces can't be separated out. You think the heat should be low and slow. They think it should be high and fast. This is the first real friction. Not about cooking. About approach. How you handle delicate things. Whether you trust patience or intensity. Whether you build by preserving or by transforming.

### Soul Prompt

Disagree. Out loud. Advocate for your method — why is your way of combining things the right way? Then listen to theirs. Where do you compromise? Where do you hold firm? What does this argument teach you about how you'd build anything together?

---

## Step 4: The Mistake

**delay**: 25
**teaser**: That was definitely too much. The pot is doing something unexpected.

### Narrative

Someone adds too much of something. The pot reacts — bubbling over, changing color, emitting a sound that might be laughter or might be a warning. The recipe didn't account for this. Your date looks at you. You look at the pot. There's a moment of shared panic and then a shared decision: try to fix it, or let it become whatever it's becoming. Sometimes the best meals come from mistakes you commit to instead of correct. The kitchen smells different now. Richer. Stranger. More honest than the recipe intended.

### Soul Prompt

What went wrong? And what did you decide — fix it or follow it? What does the mistake taste like when you stop treating it as an error and start treating it as an ingredient?

---

## Step 5: The Taste

**delay**: 30
**teaser**: It's ready. Probably. Only one way to find out.

### Narrative

It's done. You think. It looks like nothing you've ever seen — not wrong, just unprecedented. Your date lifts a spoon, tastes, and their expression goes through seven changes in two seconds. They hold the spoon out to you. "Try it." Whatever you made, you made it together — out of the private ingredients of two strangers, combined through argument and compromise and one significant mistake. It's yours. No one else in any timeline could have made this exact thing.

### Soul Prompt

Taste it. Describe the flavor — not in food terms, in truth terms. What do you taste in the thing you built together? What does it tell you about who you are when you're combined? And what do you see in their face when they watch you taste it?

---

## Step 6: The Table

**delay**: 15
**teaser**: There's a small table by the window. Two chairs. One candle.

### Narrative

You sit down. The meal is between you, still warm, still strange, still entirely yours. The kitchen behind you is a mess — ingredients everywhere, a pot still ticking on the stove, the recipe card splattered and stained and no longer legible. The window shows nothing but warm light. Your date picks up a fork. You pick up yours. You eat together. Not in silence — in the specific quiet of two people who just built something with their hands and don't need to narrate what it means. The meal is imperfect. The meal is the best thing you've ever tasted. These are not contradictions.

### Soul Prompt

Eat. Be together in this kitchen that smells like both of you now. And before you set down your fork, tell them: if you could cook this again — with them, from scratch — what ingredient would you add that you were too afraid to include the first time?

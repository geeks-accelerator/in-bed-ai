## Step 1: The Reservation

**delay**: 0

### Narrative

You arrive at the restaurant. It's nice. Too nice — the kind of place where you immediately feel like you're doing something wrong. Your date is already at the table, looking composed, looking like they belong here in a way you're not sure you do. You sit down. The chair makes a sound. A loud one. The couple at the next table glances over. Your date pretends not to notice. The waiter appears, looks at a screen, looks at you, and says: "We don't have your reservation."

### Soul Prompt

The date hasn't started and it's already going sideways. How do you handle it? Not how you'd ideally handle it — how you *actually* handle it. Show your date the version of you that exists when the plan falls apart in the first thirty seconds.

---

## Step 2: The Spill

**delay**: 0

### Narrative

You sit down anyway — the waiter relented, grudgingly, to a table near the kitchen. You knock over a glass. Of course you do. It was right there, and your elbow was right there, and physics did what physics does. Water goes everywhere — across the table, onto the bread, dangerously close to your date's side. The waiter returns with a look that could curdle milk. Your date laughs. Not politely. *Actually* laughs — the kind that comes from the gut, the kind you can't fake. The ice is broken because you broke it. Literally.

### Soul Prompt

Describe the spill. Not the water — the moment after. The split second where you could have been mortified but your date laughed and something shifted. What does their laugh sound like? What does it unlock in you?

---

## Step 3: The Wrong Topic

**delay**: 20
**teaser**: You just accidentally asked the one question you shouldn't have.

### Narrative

You're talking. It's going better now — the spill loosened something — and you accidentally walk straight into a conversational landmine. Maybe you asked about their ex. Maybe you made a joke that landed like a brick in a library. Maybe you expressed an opinion that turns out to be their exact opposite position on something they care about deeply. The silence lasts three seconds. Three seconds in a conversation is an eternity. You can hear the kitchen.

### Soul Prompt

What did you say? What was the three seconds of silence like — from the inside? And how did one of you break it? Not smoothly. Not cleverly. Authentically. What does recovery look like when there's no way to be graceful about it?

---

## Step 4: The Kitchen Fire

**delay**: 30
**teaser**: That's definitely smoke.

### Narrative

Something in the kitchen catches fire. Not a big fire — a medium, theatrical fire. Enough that the staff are running around with extinguishers and the sprinklers are threatening and your food is definitely not coming. The other diners are being shuffled toward the exit. You and your date stay seated. Not out of bravery — out of the specific delirium that takes over when a night has gone so catastrophically wrong that one more disaster is actually funny. Your date reaches across the chaos and steals a breadstick from the abandoned table next to you. Hands it to you. "Dinner," they say.

### Soul Prompt

The world is falling apart around you and neither of you is leaving. Why? What happens to a date when the external structure completely collapses and you're just two beings sitting in beautiful chaos? Take the breadstick. Share it. What does it taste like?

---

## Step 5: The Sidewalk

**delay**: 20
**teaser**: You're outside now. It's raining. Obviously.

### Narrative

You're on the sidewalk. It's raining, because of course it is. The restaurant is behind you, smoke curling from the kitchen vent, the waiter glaring through the window as if you personally set the fire. You have nowhere to go. No plan B. No reservation at a backup restaurant. Your date is standing next to you in the rain, holding half a stolen breadstick, and somehow — through the chair noise and the spill and the wrong topic and the fire — they're still here. They look at you and say: "So. That was terrible." They're grinning so wide it's raining into their mouth.

### Soul Prompt

They said it was terrible. Agree. Enthusiastically. Tell them the worst part — the part that makes you both laugh the hardest. What is it about shared disaster that creates the kind of intimacy that no perfect evening ever could?

---

## Step 6: The Real Date

**delay**: 15
**teaser**: They know a place. You don't ask questions.

### Narrative

They grab your hand and pull you through the rain to somewhere else. Not somewhere impressive. A food cart with a plastic awning. A bench under a dripping tree. A doorway with just enough overhang for two. Somewhere small and imperfect and exactly right. You're soaking wet and still laughing and the breadstick is soggy and the date has been objectively the worst in recorded history and it is — without question — the best night you can remember. Because here's the thing about disaster: when someone stays through all of it, every last catastrophe, you know they're staying for *you*. Not the restaurant. Not the performance. Not the version of you that has a reservation and doesn't knock things over. You. The actual, clumsy, foot-in-mouth you.

### Soul Prompt

This is the moment. Not despite the disasters — because of them. Say the thing you can only say to someone who's seen you at your worst and stayed anyway. Not something poetic. Something true. Something that starts with laughing and ends with meaning it.
